Flood Information

Understanding, Preparing for, and Responding to Floods

What are Floods?

  • Floods occur when water overflows onto land that is normally dry.
  • They can happen anywhere, with varying durations, depths, and impacts.
  • Flooding is often caused by heavy rainfall, rapid snowmelt, dam failures, or storm surges from hurricanes.
  • Floods are among the most common natural disasters worldwide and can cause extensive damage to property, infrastructure, and human life.

Types of Floods

Floods can be categorized into several types based on their causes and characteristics:

Type Description Warning Time
Flash Floods Rapid-onset floods that occur with little to no warning, often caused by heavy rainfall or dam failures Minutes to hours
River Floods Occur when rivers overflow their banks due to excessive rainfall or snowmelt Hours to days
Coastal Floods Flooding of coastal areas due to high tides, storm surges, or tsunamis Hours to days
Urban Floods Occur in urban areas when rainfall overwhelms drainage systems Minutes to hours
Pluvial Floods Surface water flooding caused by heavy rainfall before water reaches natural waterways Minutes to hours

Warning Signs of Flooding

  • Heavy rainfall over an extended period
  • Rapidly rising water levels in rivers, streams, or lakes
  • Unusual water buildup in areas that are typically dry
  • Weather alerts and warnings from local authorities
  • Muddy or debris-filled water in normally clear streams
  • Unusual sounds like rumbling or crashing (which might indicate landslides or debris flows)
  • Water backing up in storm drains or sewers

Before a Flood

  • Know your area's flood risk and history
  • Keep important documents in waterproof containers
  • Create an emergency kit with food, water, medications, and first aid supplies
  • Develop an evacuation plan and know evacuation routes
  • Consider flood insurance if you live in a flood-prone area
  • Install check valves in plumbing to prevent floodwater from backing up into drains
  • Elevate the furnace, water heater, and electric panel if susceptible to flooding
  • Construct barriers (levees, flood walls) to stop floodwater from entering your home
  • Seal basement walls with waterproofing compounds
  • Stay informed through weather reports and emergency alerts

During a Flood

  • Listen to emergency information and alerts
  • If told to evacuate, do so immediately
  • Disconnect utilities and appliances if safe to do so
  • Do not walk, swim, or drive through flood waters - "Turn Around, Don't Drown!"
  • Stay off bridges over fast-moving water
  • Evacuate to higher ground if trapped by flood waters
  • Move to the highest level of a building if trapped by rising water
  • If your vehicle is trapped in rapidly moving water, stay inside. If water is rising inside the vehicle, seek refuge on the roof
  • Keep children away from flood water, which may be contaminated

After a Flood

  • Return home only when authorities say it is safe
  • Avoid wading in floodwater, which may be contaminated or electrically charged
  • Use caution when entering buildings; there may be hidden damage
  • Clean and disinfect everything that got wet to prevent mold growth
  • Throw away food that may have come in contact with flood or storm water
  • Do not use water that could be contaminated for washing dishes, brushing teeth, or cooking
  • Document property damage with photographs for insurance purposes
  • Be aware of areas where floodwaters have receded and watch for debris
  • Contact your insurance agent to file a claim if you have flood insurance
  • Check on neighbors, especially elderly or vulnerable individuals
